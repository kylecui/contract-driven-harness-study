# Confidence Grading

## Grade Definitions

| Grade | Label | Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| A | High confidence | 3+ independent high-quality sources agree; no significant contradictions |
| B | Moderate confidence | 2+ sources agree; minor contradictions resolved; methodology sound |
| C | Low confidence | Single source or limited evidence; some assumptions required |
| D | Speculative | Based primarily on inference or analogy; needs validation |

## Grading Factors

### Upgrades confidence
- Multiple independent sources
- Primary data (not secondary summaries)
- Peer-reviewed or officially published
- Reproducible methodology described
- Recent and current

### Downgrades confidence
- Single source only
- Secondary or tertiary source
- Marketing material or advocacy
- Old and possibly outdated
- Methodology unclear or absent
- Author has undisclosed conflicts

## Reporting Confidence

In reports, confidence should be:
1. Stated explicitly per key finding
2. Separated from the finding itself
3. Accompanied by evidence count
4. Transparent about limitations

Example:
> **Finding**: Market will reach $X by 2028.  
> **Confidence**: C (based on single analyst report; no primary data)  
> **Evidence**: EV-000012

## When Confidence is Too Low

If all evidence for a key claim is Grade D:
- Flag as "requires further research"
- Do not present as established finding
- Move to "open questions" section
- Consider whether the claim should be in the report at all
