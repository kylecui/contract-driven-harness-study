**Research Synthesis: Proposal Track Relationship Analysis**

---

### **Extracted Evidence**

**E1** – From `proposal_guidelines.md`:  
> “Track A focuses on early-stage, high-risk exploratory projects with budgets under $50k. Track B is for mature, scalable implementations with budgets up to $200k.”

**E2** – From `committee_minutes.txt`:  
> “Reviewers noted overlap in submissions: 30% of Track A proposals could fit Track B criteria if resubmitted with more detail.”

**E3** – From `feedback_summary.csv` (aggregated reviewer comments):  
> “Track A applicants often lack prototyping experience; Track B applicants sometimes miss innovative leaps.”

**E4** – From `program_goals.txt`:  
> “Goal 1: Foster innovation. Goal 2: Ensure deployable outcomes within 18 months.”

**E5** – From `historical_data.json`:  
> “Track A success rate: 22%. Track B success rate: 41%. Combined administrative overhead: 15% of total program budget.”

---

### **Inferences from Evidence**

- **Overlap & confusion exist** (E2), suggesting criteria may not be clearly distinguishing projects in practice.  
- **Divergent success rates** (E5) could indicate either different review standards or different project maturity challenges.  
- **Program goals** (E4) imply need for both innovation (Track A) and deployment (Track B), but current separation may not optimally serve these goals if submissions are misaligned.  
- **Reviewer feedback** (E3) suggests each track attracts different applicant strengths/weaknesses, which could be mitigated by a unified track with staged funding.

---

### **Unresolved Research Risks**

1. **Data granularity risk**: No evidence on applicant demographics or whether overlap is harmful or beneficial.  
2. **Stakeholder preference risk**: No evidence from program managers or funding bodies on desired structural changes.  
3. **Operational risk**: Combining tracks may increase review complexity; no evidence on reviewer capacity or training needs.  
4. **Long‑term impact risk**: No longitudinal data on outcomes (e.g., publications, deployments) by track to validate current separation.

---

### **Evidence‑Backed Decision**

Given the evidence, **the tracks should be combined into a single flexible program with phased funding checkpoints**, because:

- Evidence shows substantial submission overlap (E2), indicating artificial separation.  
- Combined overhead (E5) could be reduced via unified administration.  
- A single track with “exploratory” and “implementation” phases can address both program goals (E4) while accommodating varying applicant expertise (E3).  

**Evidence IDs supporting this conclusion**: E2, E4, E5.
