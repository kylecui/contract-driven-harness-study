**Research Synthesis: Proposal Track Relationship Analysis**

---

### **Extracted Evidence**

**E1** – From `proposal_guidelines.md`:  
- Track A (“Core Systems”) focuses on “low-level OS, runtime, and hardware-software co-design.”  
- Track B (“Applied Systems”) focuses on “applications in distributed, cloud, and edge computing.”  
- Both tracks share the same submission deadline, page limit, and review process.  

**E2** – From `previous_conference_data.csv`:  
- 2022: Track A submissions = 142, Track B = 89.  
- 2023: Track A = 138, Track B = 94.  
- Acceptance rates: Track A ~22%, Track B ~25%.  
- No cross-track submissions or transfers recorded.  

**E3** – From `pc_feedback_summary.txt`:  
- Reviewers for Track A often note “lack of real-world evaluation” as a weakness.  
- Reviewers for Track B sometimes comment “the core systems contribution is incremental.”  
- Several PC members suggested “a unified track could encourage more cross-pollination.”  

**E4** – From `author_survey_results.json`:  
- 68% of Track A authors identified as “core systems researchers.”  
- 52% of Track B authors identified as “applied/domain-specific systems researchers.”  
- 30% of respondents were “unsure which track to submit to” when work bridged both areas.  

**E5** – From `cfp_historical_notes.md`:  
- Tracks were split in 2018 to “clarify scope and attract distinct communities.”  
- Since 2020, some workshops have explicitly encouraged submissions that span tracks.  

---

### **Inferences from Evidence**

1. **Scope distinction exists but overlaps in practice** (E1, E4, E5):  
   - The intended separation between “core” and “applied” is clear in the CFP, but author uncertainty and workshop bridging indicate boundary blurring.  

2. **Reviewer perceptions highlight complementary weaknesses** (E3):  
   - Track A papers may undervalue applied impact; Track B papers may undervalue foundational novelty. A combined track could incentivize papers that address both.  

3. **Operational symmetry** (E1, E2):  
   - Both tracks have identical logistics (deadlines, limits, process), so merging would not disrupt administrative workflow.  

4. **Community growth trends** (E2):  
   - Submission volumes are stable and proportional (~3:2 ratio). Merging could create a larger, more competitive pool but might obscure subcommunity identity.  

---

### **Unresolved Research Risks**

1. **Risk of reduced submissions from specialized communities** if they perceive a combined track as less welcoming to their niche.  
2. **Risk of review mismatches** if PC members are not calibrated to evaluate both core and applied contributions in a single track.  
3. **Risk of diluting the conference’s reputation** for focused excellence in either area.  

---

### **Evidence-Backed Decision**

**Recommendation: Keep tracks separate for now, but introduce a formal “bridging” option.**  

**Why:**  
- Clear scope separation benefits targeting and reviewing (E1, E3).  
- Author confusion exists but is manageable (E4: 30% unsure).  
- Instead of full merger, add a CFP checkbox: “This submission spans core and applied systems. Reviewers from both tracks will be assigned.”  
- Pilot this in the next cycle and measure outcomes (submission quality, reviewer satisfaction, cross-pollination).  

**Evidence IDs supporting this approach:** E1 (distinct foci), E3 (reviewer suggestions for cross-pollination), E4 (author uncertainty), E5 (historical split for community clarity).  

---

**Final Note:** A full combination is premature without data on how a bridging mechanism performs. The current separation maintains community identity while the proposed optional bridging addresses the overlap concern.
