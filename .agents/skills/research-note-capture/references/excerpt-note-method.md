# Excerpt Note Method

## Purpose

Excerpt notes preserve the raw material of research: original text, its context, and your initial interpretation. They bridge the gap between "I read something" and "I have evidence."

## Structure of a Good Excerpt Note

1. **Original text** — exact quote, not paraphrased
2. **Location** — page, section, paragraph, URL fragment, timestamp
3. **Paraphrase** — your understanding in your own words
4. **Why it matters** — connection to your research questions
5. **Tags** — topic labels for later retrieval
6. **Related questions** — what this excerpt makes you wonder

## Rules

### DO
- Copy exact text for direct quotes
- Always record location precisely enough to re-find
- Keep paraphrase clearly separate from original
- Note why this matters NOW, not later
- Link to source_id from source index

### DON'T
- Copy entire pages or sections
- Mix original text with your commentary
- Disguise abstracts as if they were body text quotes
- Skip location — "somewhere in that paper" is useless later
- Write only paraphrase without original — loses verifiability

## When to Promote to Evidence

An excerpt note becomes evidence when:
- It directly supports or contradicts a research claim
- It provides data, definition, or methodology reference
- It establishes a fact needed for the report

Use `linked_evidence_ids` to track the promotion.

## Excerpt Types

| Type | When to use |
|---|---|
| `direct_quote` | Exact words from source |
| `paraphrase` | Restated in your own words (original still recorded) |
| `data_point` | Specific number, statistic, or measurement |
| `definition` | Term definition from authoritative source |
| `method_description` | How something was done |
| `finding` | Result or conclusion from source |
