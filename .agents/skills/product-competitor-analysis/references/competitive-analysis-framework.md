# Competitive Analysis Framework for Product Teams

## 1) Objective: support strategy choices, not produce a catalog
A strong competitive analysis should change at least one product decision:

- where to compete,
- what to differentiate,
- what not to build,
- how to defend against likely reactions.

If the output only lists features, it is incomplete.

## 2) Scope definition before data collection

Define competitor universe in three rings:

1. Direct competitors (same core job, same buyer).
2. Indirect substitutes (different solution, same outcome).
3. Emerging entrants (adjacent players likely to move in).

Set explicit inclusion rules (e.g., geography, pricing tier, segment).
Record exclusion reasons to reduce selection bias.

## 3) Feature matrix design that stays useful

Avoid giant checklists. Build matrix around user-critical jobs.

Column groups:

- Core job completion capabilities
- Reliability/performance characteristics
- Integration and interoperability
- Governance, security, compliance
- Pricing and packaging
- Operational burden (setup, maintenance, support)

Scoring approach:

- Binary (present/absent) for factual capabilities.
- Ordinal (1-5) for quality dimensions with scoring rubric.
- Confidence label (high/medium/low) per row.

Each score must include evidence source and date.

## 4) Positioning map construction

Choose two axes that matter to buyer decisions, not internal preferences.

Axis examples:

- Ease of adoption vs depth of customization
- Time-to-value vs total control
- Price accessibility vs enterprise readiness

Construction steps:

1. Define axis scoring rubric.
2. Score all players with evidence.
3. Plot and annotate confidence ranges.
4. Mark crowded zones and whitespace.

Use uncertainty bands when evidence is weak.

## 5) SWOT methodology (operator version)

SWOT is useful only when tied to actions.

Rules:

- Strength/Weakness = internal capabilities.
- Opportunity/Threat = external conditions.
- No vague statements; each item needs evidence.

Convert SWOT into strategy moves:

- S-O move: exploit strength into growth window.
- W-O move: close weakness to unlock opportunity.
- S-T move: use strength to defend against threat.
- W-T move: mitigate risk or avoid exposed segment.

## 6) Competitive response prediction

Predict how incumbents may react when you launch a differentiator.

Response types:

- Feature matching (fast copy)
- Pricing pressure (discount, bundling)
- Channel lock-in (partner or ecosystem leverage)
- Messaging reframing (category narrative shift)

Estimate likelihood by:

- capability to respond,
- incentive to respond,
- response speed history.

Simple model:

Response risk = capability score x incentive score x speed score

Use 1-5 scale and monitor high-risk competitors.

## 7) Market share estimation approaches (practical)

Use triangulation, not single-source claims.

Input signals:

- public revenue disclosures,
- customer count claims (adjusted for credibility),
- job postings and hiring growth,
- traffic/download proxies,
- third-party analyst ranges.

Estimate share as range:

- conservative,
- base,
- aggressive.

Document assumptions behind each range.

## 8) Update cadence and drift control

Competitive landscapes decay quickly.

Minimum cadence:

- core matrix: monthly refresh,
- pricing/packaging: bi-weekly watch,
- major launches: ad-hoc trigger updates.

Track staleness flags when evidence is older than 90 days.

## 9) Deliverable format for decision meetings

Provide these sections in order:

1. Competitive scope and assumptions.
2. Feature matrix summary and major gaps.
3. Positioning map and whitespace opportunities.
4. SWOT-to-action table.
5. Predicted competitor responses and counters.
6. Market share range and confidence.
7. Recommended strategic options.

## 10) Example (SaaS workflow automation)
Finding: incumbents are strong in integrations, weaker in SMB onboarding, and likely to respond via pricing bundles.
Implication: compete on time-to-value, protect via onboarding moat, avoid enterprise price wars.
